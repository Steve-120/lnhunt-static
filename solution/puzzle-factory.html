The primes for this are 97 × 577 × 4241 × 40493 × 486671 × 7158271 × 125897267 × 2596426409 × 61797607633.

This is probably the most difficult one. When taking the indices of these primes, one might notice that they're unusually close to some other prime.

For example, the prime index of 40493 is 4245, which is very close to 4241. To get the answer, we take the difference between the prime index and the previous prime, then extract the letter using A1Z26.

Author’s Notes

Constructing this puzzle is actually not as hard as it looks. According to the prime number theorem, the probability of an n-digit number being prime is 1/log(n). This means if you want to generate a random n-digit number, you'd only need around log(n) tries to get a prime. To quickly determine whether a number is prime, I used sympy's isprime function.

Generating the "binary" primes like 177777101017777777 was the easiest since there are lots of freedom: the number of 7s to the left and right, and the number of leading zeros of the binary number. I randomly generate numbers of this form until I end up with a prime number.

Generating the "sequence" primes like 1234067 is a bit trickier. It involved lots of trial and error, seeing which sequences yield a nice prime or not. I also didn't want the number of terms to be too many, otherwise the prime number would be too large to factor. I first chose "nice" sequences like Lucas and Fibonacci, which forced me to have letters D and E in the answer. For the rest I could just choose any sequence of the form an2 + bn + c to force a term, which is why so many of the sequences ended up being linear or quadratic.

Generating the "numpad" primes like 7412585269 also involved some trial and error. I first drew a couple of letters on the numpad in various ways, then I listed down all the ones which gave me a prime number. Not all letters are possible with this, so I only had a list of possible letters to use for the subpuzzle. WATERSKIN happened to have all letters which yield prime numbers that can be numpad-traced.

For the other "one prime only" subpuzzles, I just bruteforce-searched a dictionary in Python to see all possible 9-letter words which would yield a prime number in that cipher.

The final star battle isn't hard to make prime. I think pretty much any star battle puzzle works. The prime should have 81 digits, so the probability of it being prime is 1/log(81) or approximately 1/4. Since there are 9! = 362880 ways to permute the digits 1-9, it's pretty much guaranteed that there's going to be a prime that works.

I'm really glad this puzzle idea hasn't been sniped before. Everytime I see a "large number" puzzle in any puzzlehunt I get a mini heart attack haha.
